I put the hulking gadget to the test during my busiest (and only) baking season of the year: the winter holidays. I whipped up meringue, pounded some pastry, beat cakes and cookies into submission, and even emulsified aioli with the Beast (my nickname for the mixer because it weighs 42 pounds!). Was it all much easier thanks to the mixer‚Äôs extra smarts?¬†No.

While I found the added features useful, the tech really got in the way of my workflow. The app, which is the only way to use certain features, isn‚Äôt well designed. I‚Äôd prefer it if everything were built into the device, maybe via a bigger screen, perhaps with touch capability, rather than needing to rely on a smartphone to access the features of this expensive gadget.

GE‚Äôs smart stand mixer is a modern update to a category ripe for disruption. Stand mixers have barely changed since my grandma used to lug one around the kitchen of the National Trust Orangery she ran in the 1980s.¬†

A stand mixer excels at tackling labor-intensive, boring mixing tasks like creaming butter, whipping cream, and mashing potatoes. GE‚Äôs new model adds Wi-Fi and Bluetooth connectivity, a digital display, a notification LED, a built-in scale, and a timer that stops mixing when it‚Äôs done.¬†

The headline trick is Auto Sense. The same applies to the Active Stir feature, which folds ingredients, slowly rotating forward and backward and pausing to mix it up without your input. The app is GE Appliances‚Äô SmartHQ app. This is the same app you use for all of the company‚Äôs connected gadgets, and it‚Äôs as good as you‚Äôd expect an appliance manufacturer app to be, which is to say, it‚Äôs not. These guided recipes work with the mixer by sending the correct time and speed for each mixing step to the device and setting the built-in scale to weigh your ingredients as you add them. I really liked these features, which take a lot of the guesswork out of baking. (When a recipe says ‚Äúmedium speed,‚Äù is that 4 or 5? No such worries here.) This smart mixer‚Äôs connected features are underbaked

As a mixer, the 7.5-quart, 540-watt Beast does everything my existing midrange 325-watt KitchenAid can do but with a more powerful motor, a bigger bowl, a slower stir setting, and a faster top speed. It literally goes to 11. My KitchenAid, which goes to 10, is nowhere near as fast.

Interestingly, the smart mixer works with front-mounted KitchenAid attachments, like the meat grinder or pasta maker. I loved the built-in scale, which is in both the base and the bowl, so you can easily use it outside of your baking tasks. After two decades of muscle memory using my KitchenAid, I was also slightly confounded by not being able to lift the mixer arm up to place the bowl; instead, you lower the bowl mechanism using two side arms, which is simple enough to do but counterintuitive for me.

As a regular mixer, the smart mixer is excellent, and besides the two features I‚Äôve mentioned, you don‚Äôt need the app or Wi-Fi to use it. The top of the mixer has a control pad with five buttons. I tried several of the guided recipes in the SmartHQ app: chocolate chip oatmeal cookies with Auto Sense for creaming the butter and sugar, a chocolate dacquoise for meringue making, a one-minute aioli for emulsifying (which requires high speeds). As an experienced ‚Äî if not accomplished ‚Äî baker, I felt the guided recipes overly complicated basic steps and were frequently unclear. Pressing a button in the app to send the weight to the mixer and then waiting for it to appear before adding the ingredients slowed me down rather than helped me, although it did mean fewer bowls to clean.

Weighing is more accurate when baking than using cup measurements, so my cookies and cakes were slightly more successful but nowhere near best in show. The meringue, however, was a disaster. I won‚Äôt blame the machine, but it certainly didn‚Äôt create a miracle.¬†

I enjoyed the Auto Sense for creaming butter and sugar; once the ingredients were in, I just pressed the button, and off it went, stopping when the mixture was perfectly creamed. I liked being able to use the built-in scale to weigh items either in the bowl or directly on the base of the mixer, and while it is a big boy machine, it does do away with an extra piece of kit in your kitchen (if you were someone who weighed your ingredients).

However, it‚Äôs finicky, and when I accidentally touched the side of the bowl when adding chocolate chips, it indicated I had 600g in there when there was barely half a cup.¬†

After two weeks of using this, I don‚Äôt feel GE has added enough value here, especially for $200 more than its closest competitor: the $600 seven-quart KitchenAid Bowl-Lift Stand Mixer. However, if you can get it on sale ‚Äî and there‚Äôs one on currently that takes it down to $600 ‚Äî it‚Äôs a lot more compelling. The built-in timer and scale alone are enough to make me want to upgrade from my current mixer. The lackluster smart features may get better, but the thing is never going to weigh less.

Serious bakers will find this more finicky than fantastic

As a new entry into an old category, the smart mixer has innovative features, but they need time to proof. I think version two of this machine will be a lot better, or hopefully, it will get smarter with over-the-air upgrades. As it stands, it‚Äôs hard to see who would need this.¬†
